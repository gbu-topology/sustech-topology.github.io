Undergraduate Geometry and Topology Seminar

  • Southern University of Science and Technology

About

This is an undergraduate student seminar on geometry and topology. This semester, our main theme will be modular forms and their applications to geometry and topology. To begin with, in order to equip ourselves with basic notions and examples of modular forms, we will be working mostly algebraically and analytically from Serre's A course in arithmetic. But here are some motivations with geometric flavors (and beyond) just for you to skim through and keep in mind.

  • Viazovska and collaborators' work on sphere packings: discrete geometry + linear algebra + Lie groups + harmonic analysis + modular forms
    Award-winning expository paper
    An undergrad thesis / reading report
  • Bringmann and collaborators' work on manifolds with group actions and relations between modular forms:
    Research paper
    An undergrad thesis / reading report
  • Pollack's recent expository paper on automorphic forms and exceptional Lie groups, starting with basics of modular forms
  • Nagloo's recent expository paper on model theory and differential equations, featuring modular forms as examples
  • One more article and its sequel, reaching out to representation theory and mathematical physics

    • Here are some further references in company with Serre's and where we are heading later per interests of participants:
  • Zagier's part 1 in the 1-2-3 of modular forms is a good point of entry (signed copy available in the Library)
  • Stein's computational approach highlights examples with SageMath (available on author's website)
  • Milne's notes combine analytic (Riemann surfaces) and algebro-geometric aspects of moduli spaces
  • Cox's book aims towards class field theory, with historical background

    • We meet on Thursdays 4:20–6:10 pm. The exact room will be updated here soon.

    Here is a course description if you wish to register for credit. Note that this semester, due to the nature of the topics, you may not need topology or differential geometry as a prerequisite to understand the material to be covered per se. On the other hand, you may lack a certain amount of intuition/motivation from such experiences as we move on.
